Underestimating time is something everyone does constantly. Not just with code, but with everything. "I'll be ready in five minutes." "This should only take an hour." "I'll finish this tonight." These are things people say with complete confidence, and it's something im guilty of as well. Most importantly, I am wrong about it more often than I'd like to admit. 


## In Coding

<img width="400px" class="img-fluid" src="../img/CodeStuck.jpg">

The thought process always goes something like this: I know what I want to do. I can picture it clearly. How hard can it be to just make it? So you sit down, give yourself a few hours, open your IDE, and start. For a while, things might go smoothly, the basics come together. It starts to look like the thing you imagined, and you think to yourself, "okay, just thirty more minutes left on this."

Then you hit the first hitch. It's small, nothing huge, but fixing it reveals another problem underneath it. And that problem leads to another problem. An hour later you're sat down debugging something you didn't even know existed when the project started.

Now the more I thought about this, the more I realized it shows up everywhere. Moving into a new place, starting a paper the night before its due, trying to learn something new. These are all the same mistakes but differently dressed. We see the end goal, but we underestimate the terrain between here and there. Programming in particular is fairly brutal about exposing this issue, as the problems are usually invisible until you run into them. 

In code, you don't know the doorframe is too small until you're already trying to push the couch through it. At least when you're moving furniture, you can see the couch and you can see the doorframe.


## Why We're So Bad At This

This phenomenon has been known for ages, it's called the [planning fallacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planning_fallacy), a term coined by Daniel Kaheman and Amos Tversky, two psychologists.  The idea is simple: people consistently underestimate how long tasks will take, even when they have experience doing similar tasks before.

The reason as best as anyone can tell, is that when we imagine doing something, we imagine the best case scenario. We picture ourselves working smoothly, without interruptions, without unexpected problems. We don't picture the bug that takes two hours to find. We don't picture getting stuck on something that turns out to be an issue with a workaround buried in a three year old stack overflow post. In other words, we plan for the version of events where everything goes right. And everything almost never goes entirely right.

## So, What Helps?

I don't think this problem can be fully solved, but there are things that make it better. Breaking work into smaller pieces and estimating those instead, it's harder to be wildly wrong about a small task than a large one. "How long will this whole project take" is a question your brain can't answer well. "How long will this one function take to implement" is much easier to quantify, and when you add up enough small estimates, the total tends to be more honest than one big guess.

Similarly, you can try to take your big time estimate, and double it. Not because you are slow or bad at what you're doing, but because generally, the estimates you make don't account for issues that occur in reality. 

## Okay, So What?

I still underestimate things. I always will, but I've gotten a little better at catching myself in the moment. 

The hour that turns into a week isn't a failure. It's just a reminder that the gap between imagining something and actually doing it is where all the real work is. And that gap, no matter how experienced you get, never fully closes.
It's just part of the job.
